## Section 1 — Scrap Business From Zero

Collecting waste materials and selling them to be reused. When you throw away an old phone or broken fan, someone collects it, sorts it, sells it to a factory that melts it down and makes new products. That entire chain is the scrap business.

### The Complete Chain — Who Does What

```
Level 1 — Waste Generator
Normal people, offices, factories, shops
Throw away old electronics, metal, plastic, paper

Level 2 — Kabadiwala (Doorstep Collector)
Comes to your apartment
Buys old newspaper, broken appliances, plastic bottles by weight
Pays ₹10-50 per kg depending on material
Earns ₹20,000-50,000/month — bottom of the chain

Level 3 — Local Scrap Dealer
Kabadiwala sells everything here
Dealer sorts materials — copper separate, aluminum separate
Buys cheap from kabadiwala, sells slightly higher to traders
Earns ₹1-5L/month profit

Level 4 — Trader / Aggregator
Buys in bulk from 50-100 small dealers across city
Sells in larger quantities to recyclers
Pure arbitrage — buy copper at ₹480/kg, sell at ₹510/kg
Profit example: ₹30/kg × 5,000kg = ₹1.5L per deal
Does NOT process anything

Level 5 — Recycler / Processor
Receives large quantities from traders
Actually processes material — shreds plastic, melts metal, dismantles electronics
Extracts pure material and sells to factories
Earns ₹5-50L/month depending on size

Level 6 — Factory / Manufacturer
Buys processed scrap material
Uses as raw material to manufacture new products
Cheaper than buying virgin material
```

### Material Prices

| Material | Where It Comes From | Price | Who Buys |
|---|---|---|---|
| Copper | Old wires, motors, AC units | ₹500-700/kg | Electrical factories |
| Aluminum | Cans, window frames, old bikes | ₹120-150/kg | Casting factories |
| Steel/Iron | Old vehicles, machinery | ₹30-45/kg | Steel mills |
| Plastic | Bottles, packaging | ₹10-40/kg | Plastic granule factories |
| Paper | Newspapers, boxes | ₹8-15/kg | Paper mills |
| E-waste | Old phones, laptops, TVs | Varies by device | Authorized recyclers |
| Battery | Car batteries, UPS | ₹80-120/kg | Lead smelters |

### What Is E-Waste — Why It Is Special

E-waste is any electronic device that is discarded. Old mobile phones, laptops, televisions, refrigerators, ACs, washing machines.

**Valuable materials inside:** Gold, silver, copper, palladium inside circuit boards. One ton of old mobile phones contains more gold than one ton of gold ore from a mine.

**Toxic materials inside:** Lead, mercury, cadmium. If dumped in landfill these chemicals leak into soil and water. This is why government created strict rules — E-Waste Management Rules 2022. Only authorized recyclers can process e-waste legally.

### What Is EPR — Extended Producer Responsibility

If your company MAKES or SELLS electronics or plastic products in India, you are RESPONSIBLE for collecting and recycling a percentage of what you sell every year. Samsung sells 1 crore phones in India. By law they must ensure a percentage of old Samsung phones get properly recycled. They cannot just sell and forget.

### What Is A PRO — Producer Responsibility Organization

```
Samsung says to PRO:
"I need proof that 50,000 tons of e-waste was recycled
on my behalf this year. Here is ₹10 crore. Arrange it."

PRO says to recyclers:
"We will send you e-waste material.
You recycle it and give us certificates
proving you recycled X kg."

PRO goes back to Samsung:
"Here are your certificates.
Show these to the government for EPR compliance."
```

### What Is CPCB and SPCB

**CPCB** — Central Pollution Control Board. National government body. Sets rules for e-waste recycling nationally.

**SPCB** — State Pollution Control Board. Each state has one. Telangana has TSPCB. They give licenses to recyclers and collect quarterly reports.

**What recyclers must submit to SPCB every quarter — this is NOT ITR filing:**
- How many kg of each material did you receive
- Where did it come from
- How much did you process
- What was your output
- Where did the output go

Currently recyclers prepare this manually in Excel or Word and submit physical copies. Very painful. They pay CA or consultant ₹5,000-10,000 per quarter to prepare it.

### How PRO Gets E-Waste — Collection Network

PRO does NOT collect from kabadiwala chain. PRO gets material from REGISTERED COLLECTION CENTERS ONLY for valid EPR certificates.

- **Registered electronics stores** — Croma, authorized dealers. Every device collected is documented.
- **Corporate bulk collection** — TCS replacing 500 laptops. PRO collects with proper paperwork. TCS gets disposal certificate.
- **Collection camps** — PRO organizes e-waste events in apartment complexes. Everything documented.
- **Municipality bins** — Official CPCB registered collection points.

> Kabadiwala has no registration, no documentation. CPCB does not accept certificates based on informal material. The certificate becomes invalid. This is why the 7 lakh ton certificate fraud gap exists.

### Multiple Material Chains Exist

```
PLASTIC CHAIN:
Household/Factory → Kabadiwala → Plastic scrap dealer
→ Plastic granule maker → Plastic product manufacturer

METAL CHAIN:
Factory/Demolition/Old vehicles → Scrap dealer
→ Metal trader → Metal recycler/Smelter → Steel mill

PAPER CHAIN:
Household/Office → Kabadiwala → Paper scrap dealer → Paper mill

BATTERY CHAIN:
Cars/UPS/Inverters → Mechanic/Electrician
→ Battery dealer → Lead smelter → New battery manufacturer

SOFTWARE OPPORTUNITY BY MATERIAL:
E-waste formal chain   ← BIGGEST (most regulated, EPR strict)
Plastic formal chain   ← SECOND BIGGEST (EPR rules just started)
Battery formal chain   ← GROWING FAST (EV batteries coming)
Metal and Paper        ← Very little opportunity (least regulated)
```

```
Old sales = huge EPR target
New formal infrastructure = small actual capacity
```

### How Brands Fill The 7 Lakh Ton Gap Today

| Method | What Happens | Legal Status |
|---|---|---|
| Certificate Trading | PRO buys floating certificates from recyclers for material already processed from unknown sources. Assigns to brand's EPR account. | Technically legal but very grey |
| Channelizing Informal Material | Informal trader has 10,000 tons from kabadiwala. PRO buys it, creates backdated documentation, sends to authorized recycler, gets certificate. | Illegal |
| Inflated Certificates | Recycler actually processed 1,000 tons. Certificate says 5,000 tons. PRO accepts. Brand submits to CPCB. | Fraud |
| Pay The Fine | Some brands calculate: fine cost vs certificate cost. If fine is cheaper, just pay it. | Legal but expensive |

**Q4 — What is this statement file report — is that ITR filing?**

No, it is NOT income tax filing. It is a completely separate environmental compliance requirement. Authorized recyclers registered under E-Waste Management Rules 2022 must submit quarterly reports to State Pollution Control Board showing exactly: how many kg of each material received, where it came from, how much processed, what was output, where output went. They either ignore it and get notices, or pay someone ₹5,000-10,000 per quarter to prepare it manually. Genuine pain point but only for authorized registered recyclers — not small kabadiwala.

**Q5 — Currently there is local association for recyclers where they get price of different materials and post in WhatsApp — they are happy, so why they pay?**

Your real market is the authorized recycler and above who is under regulatory pressure.

**Q7 — Give detail about scrap business — no idea what it is, give in English**

Covered completely in Section 1 of this document. The scrap business runs through: Household → Kabadiwala → Local Scrap Dealer → Trader/Aggregator → Recycler/Processor → Factory/Manufacturer. E-waste is special because it contains both valuable metals (gold, copper, palladium) AND toxic materials (lead, mercury, cadmium). Government created EPR rules requiring companies that sell electronics to be responsible for recycling them. PROs sit between brands and recyclers managing this compliance chain.

---

**Q8 — So here how does PRO get e-waste that they send to recyclers?**

PRO builds a collection network through: Registered collection centers (Croma, corporate offices, all CPCB registered), Corporate bulk collection (TCS replacing 500 laptops — PRO collects with proper paperwork), Collection camps (PRO organizes events in apartment complexes), Municipality bins (official CPCB registered bins). PRO does NOT collect from kabadiwala chain for valid EPR certificates because kabadiwala has no documentation and CPCB does not accept certificates based on informal material.

---

**Q9 — So PRO gets from centers not from kabadiwala, local dealers, medium trader, large trader?**

Correct. PRO gets material from REGISTERED COLLECTION CENTERS ONLY for valid EPR certificates. The kabadiwala chain is informal and unverified. CPCB will not accept certificates based on informally collected material. This is the exact reason the 7 lakh ton certificate fraud gap exists — brands need 12 lakh tons of certificates but formal chain only provides 5 lakh tons of real verified material.

---

**Q10 — Means there are only these two chains?**

No. There are multiple material chains — e-waste, plastic, metal, paper, battery each have their own separate flow from household to factory. Each chain has both formal (documented, regulated, certified) and informal (cash, no paperwork, no compliance) versions. E-waste and battery are most regulated. Metal and paper are least regulated. Your software opportunity is only in formal chains because informal chains have no compliance burden and will never pay for software.

---

**Q11 — What does Samsung do for the gap of 45,000 tons?**

Samsung and PROs fill the gap through four methods: Certificate trading — buying floating certificates from recyclers for material already processed from unknown sources. Channelizing informal material with backdated documentation creating paper trail. Inflated certificates — recycler claims 5,000 tons when they actually processed 1,000 tons. Paying the fine if it is cheaper than arranging certificates. All of these methods are getting impossible as CPCB builds digital cross-checking — this is the core of your opportunity.

Formal chain only. Your customers — authorized recycler, PRO, brand — are all in the formal chain. You ignore 69% of volume (informal) and focus on the 31% (formal) that has regulatory pressure and willingness to pay.
